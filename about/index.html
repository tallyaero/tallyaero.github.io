About TallyAero

TallyAero exists to
eliminate plane crashes.

After every accident, investigators pull everything together — the pilot's history, the weather, the aircraft, the route, the decisions — and show exactly how it all fit together to produce that outcome. The report gets written. And then it sits there.

We do that before the crash in order to prevent it.

No crash happens in isolation. But that's how aviation manages its data — in silos, disconnected, reactive.

No crash has a single cause. No solution will have a single fix. We're attacking it on every front — flight logging, training and proficiency, preflight risk identification, maintenance tracking, currency monitoring — all connected, all talking to each other, all aimed at one outcome.

Zero crashes.

Loss of control in flight. VFR into IMC. Fuel mismanagement. Controlled flight into terrain. These aren't mysteries. The NTSB has been writing the same probable cause reports for decades. The #1 killer in general aviation — loss of control — happens once every four days in the United States. Pilots enter weather they shouldn't. They run out of fuel they should have planned for. They stall on base-to-final in a pattern they've flown a hundred times.

Every one of these accidents has a trail of data that, if connected, would have flagged the risk before the flight ever left the ground. But that data lives in silos — the logbook doesn't talk to the weather. The training record doesn't talk to the flight plan. The maintenance history doesn't talk to the dispatch. Nobody sees the full picture until the NTSB puts it together after the crash.

We've seen this firsthand

Among our founders are military-trained instructors and an A&P mechanic. Between us we've watched friends, peers, and coworkers killed or nearly killed — not in combat, but during administrative portions of flight. Systems malfunctions. Misunderstanding of the aircraft or the procedures. Maintenance that should have been caught. All completely avoidable.

The same thing happens in general aviation every week. A student pilot who wasn't ready for the conditions. A private pilot who hadn't flown in months and didn't realize how rusty they were. A CFI who signed off an endorsement without checking the actual hours. A school that dispatched an aircraft with a known squawk because the maintenance log was in someone's email.

We are tired of watching people die for reasons that data could have prevented.

We looked at the market and saw the same story repeated for twenty years. Flight school software that solves one problem — scheduling, or billing, or the logbook — and ignores everything else. Logbook apps that are glorified spreadsheets with a nicer font. Platforms that want a sales call before they'll show you pricing. Products re-polished over and over with zero real movement, zero meaningful change, zero advancement in what's actually possible.

Fluffy apps. Meaningless features. Nobody connecting the dots. Nobody asking the hard question: why are we still crashing at the same rate we were crashing twenty years ago?

The tools exist. The data exists. Someone just has to build something that actually uses it.

Training quality is going the wrong direction

While the industry ships the same products, the quality of pilot training is actively declining. The people who will one day fly you and your family across the country are being trained by increasingly inexperienced instructors in a system that's about to get weaker.

The private pilot checkride — the most foundational exam in aviation — surpassed the CFI-Initial as the most-failed practical test in 2022 and has stayed there since. Over one in four private pilot applicants fail on their first attempt. The primary driver: experienced CFIs are being drained into the airlines, replaced by 200-300 hour instructors who are time-building, not teaching. Those instructors produce weaker students, some of whom become the next generation of CFIs. The cycle accelerates.

And now the FAA is considering making it easier.

The Part 141 "modernization"

In April 2026, a 471-page report was published under FAA Docket FAA-2024-2531 proposing a major overhaul of Part 141 pilot school regulations. The public was given ten days to comment on it. The report was written by the National Flight Training Alliance — an industry group primarily composed of the flight schools that would benefit from reduced oversight.

Among the proposals: replace the 80% first-attempt pass-rate requirement — the only objective quality metric in Part 141 — with subjective "quality system" evaluations. Move oversight out of local FSDO offices (the inspectors who actually walk into your school) into a centralized bureaucracy. Expand simulation credit so fewer hours are spent actually flying. Grant chief instructors more autonomy with less accountability.

We've seen this before. When the FAA modernized Part 147 (A&P mechanic schools) in 2022, they eliminated all seat-time requirements. New certificates jumped 32%. The mechanic shortage persists, and long-term quality data doesn't exist. The same playbook is now being applied to pilot training: increase throughput, hope quality follows.

What we think about training

We've instructed at Part 61 and Part 141 schools. We've seen students pushed through stage checks they weren't ready for. We've seen endorsements signed because the schedule demanded it, not because the student earned it. We've watched students get their certificates who we wouldn't trust to fly our families across the state, let alone across the country.

The answer to a pilot shortage is not weaker training. It's better tools. Tools that actually track whether a student is ready — not based on a CFI's gut, but based on real data from every flight they've ever logged. Tools that flag when an instructor's students are consistently underperforming. Tools that connect the training record to the logbook to the checkride so that the 12,000 annual cancelled checkrides become a thing of the past.

TallyAero doesn't lower the bar. We build the systems that make the bar achievable.

It starts with the logbook

The pilot's logbook is the most powerful dataset in aviation that nobody treats seriously. Every flight. Every hour. Every aircraft. Every airport, every condition, every instructor, every endorsement. It's the complete record of a pilot's experience — and right now it sits in a paper book or a dumb app that does nothing with it.

TallyAero starts there and builds everything on top of it.

The logbook feeds the school's training records. The training records feed dispatch. Dispatch feeds maintenance. Maintenance feeds the trust chain. DashTwo AI analyzes every regulatory question against the pilot's actual experience. The DPE portal validates checkride readiness from real data — not a CFI's best guess.

And all of it — every flight profile, every weather briefing, every routing decision, every maintenance record, every training gap — can be analyzed against NTSB accident records and real-world crash patterns to produce risk assessments at a level that has never existed in general aviation.
